Windtunnel

Rehearse a price change against 10,000 simulated customers — before real ones feel it.

What is this, in plain English? Pilots practise landings in a simulator before flying a real plane. But when a company raises prices for millions of customers, it usually just picks a number in a meeting and hopes. Windtunnel builds a crowd of simulated customers based on a company's real customer reviews, then tries the price change on them thousands of times to predict what would happen — including which customers would leave. SegmentA group of similar customers (e.g. "price-sensitive" vs "quality-focused") who react differently.
ChurnCustomers who quit because of the change.
Monte Carlo simulationRunning the same scenario many times with slight randomness, to get a range of likely outcomes instead of one guess.
Confidence band"Most likely somewhere between X and Y." Honest about uncertainty instead of pretending to know one exact number.

Why this matters

The thing being compared here isn't consulting fees — it's the cost of the decision that turned out wrong. Companies make million-rupee pricing, policy and product calls on spreadsheets and gut, because there's nowhere to rehearse. As simulating realistic customers becomes cheap, a "rehearsal room for decisions" becomes possible for the first time. The trust is earned by publishing predictions against decisions whose real outcomes are already known — a track record a copycat can't download.
