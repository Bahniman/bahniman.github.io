The Trust Layer for the
Agent Economy

AI agents are beginning to spend money, certify work, hold institutional memory, and transact — and the trust infrastructure for all of it is missing. These are five working prototypes of that missing layer, each with a live demo and an explicit business model.

Authority — who allowed this agent to act? Reality — did the physical work actually happen? Memory — who owns what the AI has learned? Commerce — who is actually buying? Consequences — what happens if we do this?

The prototypes

🛰️

GroundTruth

Verification-backed invoice financing for public works. AI + an accountable engineer certify completed work; a bank advances 60% by the next morning against the verified receivable, instead of the contractor waiting 6–18 months for the treasury.

Run the money-flow demo →
🛡️

Surety

The authorization and liability rail for AI agents: signed spending mandates, in-line enforcement, human approval above thresholds, and a tamper-evident action log — the compliance layer that unblocks enterprise agent deployment.

Watch an agent get caught →
🏛️

Heirloom

Organizational memory as an asset the firm owns: role-scoped answers with sources, knowledge that survives partner exits, and an open export format so switching AI vendors never means corporate amnesia.

Ask as three different roles →
🚪

Turnstile

Commerce analytics for the agent era: detect the AI shoppers in your traffic, stop declining their orders as "fraud", attribute their revenue, and serve them a machine-readable storefront that converts.

Run a day of traffic →
🌪️

Windtunnel

A rehearsal room for irreversible decisions: simulate a price change against thousands of customers modeled from real behavioral data, see the outcome distribution and the breaking segment — before any real customer feels it.

Rehearse a price change →
🧩

Why these five together

Agents acting in the world create one trust vacuum with five faces: authority, physical reality, memory, commerce, and consequences. One thesis, five working answers — each independently a company, together a portfolio.
