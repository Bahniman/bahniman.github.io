GroundTruth

Watch a road measurement become money — in days, not months.

What is this, in plain English? When a contractor builds a government road in India, an engineer measures the work by hand in a paper register, and the contractor then waits 6–18 months to be paid while that register crawls through approvals. GroundTruth replaces the register: photos and AI measure the work, the engineer signs it digitally, and that signed record is solid enough for a bank to pay the contractor within two days (the bank collects from the government later). TermWhat it actually means
Measurement BookThe physical register where a government engineer hand-writes how much work was done. Every payment depends on it.
EvidenceGeotagged photos/drone captures of the site — proof with location and time stamped in.
Dual-key certificateTwo locks on one door: the AI's evidence AND the engineer's signature. Neither alone can release money.
ReceivableMoney someone owes you. Here: what the government owes the contractor for certified work.
DiscountingA bank pays you ~98-99% of that money today and collects the full amount later. You get cash now; the bank earns the difference.
Tamper-evident ledgerA record where entries are mathematically linked — editing any one visibly breaks the link.

Why this matters

An estimated ₹1–3 lakh crore is stuck in delayed payments and disputes on Indian public projects; disputes take 7–8 years to resolve; small contractors borrow at 18–24% just to survive the wait — and the government overpays because bids price all that in. The central government already runs an electronic measurement book (proof the direction works); GroundTruth completes it with the two layers nobody built: independent evidence, and same-week financing. The same "AI observes, human signs, money moves" pattern then extends to insurance claims, crop-loss assessment, and trade documents worldwide.
