GroundTruth

Verified work becomes next-morning working capital.

The business problem, precisely. A contractor finishes government work, submits the details — and then waits 6 to 18 months while a paper measurement register crawls through human verification, billing, audit, and treasury release. The work is done; the money is dead. Contractors bridge the gap at 18–24% informal rates, or simply can't take the next project. GroundTruth changes what the submission produces: instead of an entry in a register, it produces a verified, bank-grade e-invoice — evidence-backed, engineer-signed, tamper-proof. Against that instrument, a partner bank advances 60% of the contracted amount the next morning, holds 40% as its safety net, collects the full payment from the treasury whenever it arrives, and releases the balance minus financing charges. The contractor is liquid in a day and can start the next project. The bank holds near-sovereign paper. And the more reliable our verification gets, the higher the advance rate banks are willing to pay.

The flywheel: reliability buys a higher advance

The 40% holdback exists because the bank prices verification risk. Every certified invoice that settles cleanly is training data and actuarial history — and as the model's dispute rate falls, the bank can safely advance more. Drag the reliability score:

Jargon decoder
TermMeaning
Measurement BookThe physical register where a government engineer hand-writes completed work. Every payment today depends on it.
ReceivableMoney contractually owed to you. Here: what the treasury owes the contractor for certified work.
Advance / holdbackThe bank pays 60% now (advance) and keeps 40% (holdback) as its buffer until the treasury settles.
Invoice discountingGetting most of an invoice's value early from a bank, which collects the full amount later. Standard trade finance, applied to a payer (government) that never defaults but always delays.
Schedule of Rates (SoR)The government's official price list per unit of work.
Dual-key certificateMachine evidence + accountable human signature. Neither alone moves money.

Where this goes

India first: state PWDs process lakhs of contractor bills on paper, with an estimated ₹1–3 lakh crore stuck in delayed payments and disputes, and CPWD's own e-measurement system proving the government wants this journey. Then the same primitive — machine observes, accountable human signs, verified instrument moves money — prices insurance claims, agricultural lending, and trade documents. Verification is the last analog industry; every vertical it digitizes unlocks a financing market on top.
